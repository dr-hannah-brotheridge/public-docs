**United States**

In a nationally representative US sample of older adults with moderately severe dementia (incident cases 2012-2016), 64% received care at home, 19% in residential care, and 17% in nursing facilities. 

This study used the National Health and Aging Trends Study, which actively screened participants for dementia rather than relying solely on diagnosed cases.

People living at home were 2-5 times more likely to be from disadvantaged populations and had more medical needs (71% reported bothersome pain vs 60% in residential care or 59% in nursing homes). 

**Canada (Ontario)**

A population-based study of 108,757 community-dwelling older adults with newly diagnosed dementia found that at 5 years after diagnosis, 24.4% remained alive in the community and 20.5% were living in long-term care. Of the 55.1% who died, about half (27.9%) were admitted to long-term care before death. 

**Norway**

Among 2,938 home-dwelling persons with dementia followed for up to 10.6 years, approximately 4 years after diagnosis, the probability of living in a nursing home peaked at 19%, with half of participants still living at home. Median time from diagnosis to nursing home admission among those admitted was 2.28 years. 

**Global systematic review and meta-analysis**

A 2025 BMJ systematic review of 261 studies across 352,990 participants found: 

* Median time to nursing home admission was 3.3 years (IQR 1.9-4.0)  
* 13% were admitted in the first year after diagnosis  
* 57% were admitted within 5 years  
* About one-third of remaining life expectancy was lived in nursing homes

**a. Undiagnosed dementia on admission**

**Approximately 55-65% of patients with dementia are undiagnosed at hospital admission** in most studies. An Irish multi-hospital study found that only 35.6% of patients with dementia had a previous diagnosis, meaning **64.4% were undiagnosed**.

A UK study found that among patients with delirium (who commonly have underlying dementia), **38% had previously undiagnosed cognitive impairment**. 

Another UK study reported that **64% of dementia cases were undiagnosed** before hospital presentation. 

In New Zealand, a 2024 capture-recapture analysis estimated that **47.8% of all dementia cases** in the country are not present in any health datasets, suggesting substantial underdiagnosis. 

In the US, studies show that among community-dwelling older adults with probable dementia, **39.5% were undiagnosed** based on Medicare claims, with an additional 19.2% diagnosed but unaware of their diagnosis. 

**b. Living in the community (not in residential care)**

**The majority of hospitalised dementia patients come from the community**. A European study found that **18.5% of people with dementia living at home** experienced at least one hospital admission within 3 months, compared to 13.8% of those in nursing homes. 

A US study of potentially preventable hospitalizations found that **86.0% of hospitalized patients with dementia were admitted from the community**, though only 32.7% were discharged back to the community. 

Among community-dwelling older adults newly diagnosed with dementia in Canada, approximately **75% remained in the community** at baseline, though within 5 years, only 24.4% remained alive in the community, with 20.5% having moved to long-term care and 55.1% having died.

**c. Lacking appointed EPOA/advance directives**

**The majority of dementia patients lack advance care directives**, though rates of enduring power of attorney (EPOA) or enduring guardianship are somewhat better. An Australian study of carers reported that among people with dementia, **only 47% had completed an advance care directive**, though 89% had appointed an Enduring Power of Attorney and 93% had appointed an Enduring Guardian. 

A US tertiary hospital study found that **advance directives were frequently absent** in hospitalized dementia patients, and goals of care were infrequently discussed despite the lack of advance directives. 

An Australian medical record audit across hospital and community settings found that only **2.8% of patients had an advance care directive**, with only 1% being legally binding, and **3.9% had appointed an Enduring Guardian**. 

A Canadian rural memory clinic study found that among patients presenting with cognitive concerns, 73.7% had a Will, 65.3% had a Power of Attorney, but only **22.1% had an Advanced Healthcare Directive**. 

These statistics highlight significant gaps in dementia recognition and advance care planning across healthcare systems, with most dementia remaining undiagnosed at hospital admission, the majority of patients coming from community settings, and substantial proportions lacking formal advance care planning documentation.

**Electronic health record challenges:**

* Advance care planning information is found in multiple inconsistent locations within EHRs: 69% in progress notes, 43% in scanned documents, and 34% in problem lists, often with considerable inconsistencies between locations.   
* A review noted that "advance directives are often not available when needed," and their impact on care received is unclear.   
* Even when forms are completed, there are significant delays in getting them into the EHR. One study found that while an intervention increased advance care planning form completion, only 37% were completed during the initial visit; the remaining 63% were later scanned into the EHR. Additionally, about 9% of completed forms were never scanned into the EHR at all.
