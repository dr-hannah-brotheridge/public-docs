### **Refined from the Kete ID feedback and engagement survey**

This dataset includes feedback from 22 participants, including doctors, nurses, family caregivers, and facility managers, primarily based in Christchurch.

Here is a summary of the key insights and feedback gathered from the responses:

### **1\. Current Use of Identification & Tracking**

* **Existing Tools:** Participants noted using various existing solutions, including **MedicAlert**, **Apple AirTags**, **GPS trackers**, **Wandersearch**, and the **Life 360 app**.  
* **Common Failures:** Feedback highlighted several issues with current methods:  
  * **User Error:** Individuals often forget to put on the device or forget how to use/press it when in trouble.  
  * **Technical Barriers:** GPS trackers can be expensive or difficult to set up without a strong technical background.  
  * **Sensory Issues:** Many patients with dementia find jewelry or bracelets irritating and may remove them.

### **2\. Safety and Communication Challenges**

* **Wandering:** This was the most frequently cited safety concern among family caregivers and facility managers.  
* **Identification in Emergencies:** Clinicians often struggle when patients are sent to the hospital without documentation of their goals of care, advance care plans (ACP), or activated Enduring Power of Attorney (EPOA).  
* **Communication Barriers:** Challenges include hearing loss, loss of language skills, and the patient's inability to express their thoughts or situation clearly.

### **3\. Feedback on the Kete ID Concept**

* **Sentiment:** The majority of respondents expressed strong support, using terms like "Love it" or "Sounds useful".  
* **Key Suggestions for the Prototype:**  
  * **Form Factor:** Respondents suggested non-jewelry options such as **brooches**, **sewn labels**, or attachments for **mobility aids**.  
  * **Durability:** The device should ideally be waterproof and suitable for 24/7 wear to become "part of them".  
  * **Privacy & Access:** There were concerns regarding who can access the data. Some suggested restricted access for healthcare workers via a secure app or password-protected system.

### **4\. Recommended Information for Responders**

Participants suggested that scanning the ID should ideally reveal:

* **Core Details:** Name, DOB, NHI number, and home address.  
* **Emergency Contacts:** Primary and secondary Next of Kin (NOK) details.  
* **Medical & Care Plans:** Current medications, allergies, resuscitation wishes, and a link to their Advance Care Plan or SGoC.  
* **Behavioral Guidance:** Tips on how to interact with the person (e.g., "speak slowly and clearly") and whether they have a tendency toward aggression or wandering.

**Based off this, how is Kete ID different to:**

**Life360:**   
Life360 is a tracker for anything i.e. pets, bags, keys, people and uses GPS. They also have crash detection, lost phone coverage and a location history. They send SOS alerts to contacts and include crime reports. Can ring the phone from the tracker. Come in the form of a tile or keyring. Paid $50-150. Lasts 3 years battery.

**Wandersearch:**  
NZ-based used for anyone at risk of going missing. Uses a small device that uses radio-frequency signals worn by that person that trained people can detect. Do not use cellular networks so work anywhere, does not need charging. Wrist device/pendant or keyring. Lasts 3-6 months battery. Showerproof. Needs a CC308+ meter to check it works every week or so. Have to call the police to have it tracked. Funded in NZ.

**AppleAir Tags:**  
Apple exclusive. Needs bluetooth. Used to locate personal items. Lasts 1 year battery. Water resistant. Check your phone to see where the tag is. Paid $50 or so.

**MedicAlert:**  
A bracelet/pendant that stores medical information accessible for first responders. Can store allergies, advanced care plans, implants/devices, health conditions, meds, contact info. Paid membership at $7 a month plus cost of bracelet ($15-500). Taken from clinically validated sources. Can let emergency contacts know when used. Health info has to come from a health provider. Can be updated. Has EPOA, ACP, Jehovah’s Witness, DNR, Drug trials if wanted. Essentially first responders call their hotline to access records.

**How will it be novel?**

**Kete ID** is to build the "Dementia-Specific" version that MedicAlert currently isn't:

1. **Direct-to-User Info:** Let the "finder" see immediate contact and behavioral tips. Instead of having to call the medic-alert 24/7 hotline to gain access which can take time.  
2. **Tiered Access:** Perhaps the public sees "Call my daughter" while paramedics/doctors use a professional login on the same page to see the DNR/ACP. Having some restrictions on access may be that only health care workers can see ACP but first responders can access emergency contacts/behavioural advice.  
3. **Passive Form Factors:** Move away from the "bracelet" model toward integrated clothing labels or patches that the patient cannot easily remove. For example a brooch, sewn on badge/label, sticker on mobility aid etc.

**Helpful feedback**

There are four distinct groups of patients and Kete ID might struggle to help all of them:

* **Group A (The "Crisis" Patients):** These are people whose dementia wasn't known until a crisis hit. They wouldn't have a Kete ID because they weren't diagnosed yet.  
* **Group B (The "Carer Stress" Patients):** These patients are admitted because their family can't cope anymore. Knowing their ACP or EPOA doesn't change the fact that they have nowhere else to go right now.  
* **Group C (The "Acute Event" Patients):** If they have a broken hip or a stroke, they *must* be admitted. The ID helps with paperwork (finding the EPOA), but it doesn't prevent the "hospital burden."  
* **Group D (The "Wanderers"):** This is the target, but families who are organised enough to set up a Kete ID are usually already managing well and might prefer a **GPS tracker** (like Life360/AirTags) over a passive ID.

### **Technical & Ethical Hurdles**

* **The Data Entry Gap:** Who actually uploads the info? If the patient can't do it, and the EPOA isn't "activated" yet, there is a legal/privacy grey area regarding who is authorised to put health data into your system.  
* **The "Buy-In" Problem:** If it requires GPs or Nurses to upload data, it adds to their already overloaded "admin" plate.  
* **Trust in the Plan:** Would a paramedic actually leave a confused person in the community just because a tag said so? They might still default to "Hospital \= Safe."

The most valuable part is the "Work Backwards" suggestion:

> *"Work backwards from admission... considering if the information on the Kete ID would have prevented the admission if it had all been available at the time."*

### **How to Respond / Pivot**

Prove **ROI (Return on Investment)** for the hospital. 

**Refining the "Target":** Maybe Kete ID isn't for *all* dementia patients. Maybe it is specifically for the **"Borderline"** patients who are at high risk of "Social Admissions" but don't have an acute medical need.

* **The "Handover" Tool:** Admit that it won't prevent *all* admissions, but argue that it will **speed up discharges**. If the Gen Med team can see the EPOA and "Functional Baseline" on Day 1 instead of Day 5, that's 4 days of bed-space saved.  
* **Validation Study:** Offer to do a "Retrospective Audit" of 20-30 recent dementia admissions at your hospital. Look at the notes and ask: *"If I had known \[X\] via an ID tag, could we have diverted this patient or discharged them faster?"*

**How to improve Kete ID so it is more useful:**

### **1\. Shift the Target: From "Individual" to "Institutional Handover"**

Instead of marketing to individuals in the community (Group D), market to **Rest Homes and Aged Care Facilities.**

* **The Problem:** When a rest home sends a resident to the ED, the paperwork (ACP, Medication list, EPOA contact) often doesn't travel with them, or it gets lost in the triage pile.  
  *Technically not true, the rest home has a paper folder with all of this information.*  
* **The Kete ID Fix:** The ID becomes the **"Digital Passport"** for every resident. The facility pays you a subscription to ensure that if a resident is transferred, their *entire* clinical and social baseline is instantly scannable by the ED.  
* **Business Edge:** Facilities will pay to reduce the time their staff spends on the phone with the hospital and to ensure their residents aren't given medical treatment that contradicts their ACP.

### **2\. Focus on "The First 15 Minutes" of Triage**

If you can’t stop the admission, focus on **optimising the admission.**

* **The Modification:** Add a **"Triage Summary"** to the top of the digital profile. This should be a 3-point bulleted list specifically for ED staff:  
  1. **Baseline Cognition:** "Usually oriented to person/place, but not time." (Prevents unnecessary delirium workups).  
  2. **Agitation Triggers:** "Becomes combative with needles; requires distraction." (Prevents restraints).  
  3. **Discharge Destination:** "Lives at \[Facility Name\]—they have 24/7 nursing and can take him back immediately once cleared."  
* **Value:** This turns Kete ID into a **clinical efficiency tool**.

### **3\. Solve the "Who Uploads it?" (The Family-Led "Living Record")**

GPs are too busy.

* **The Modification:** Make the platform **Family-Managed but Clinically Verified.**  
  * The family uploads the "Social Profile" (hobbies, calming techniques, contacts).  
  * The family uploads a photo of the **GP-signed ACP**.  
  * The system displays a "Verified" badge only when a document with a clinical signature is present.  
* **Business Edge:** This removes the GP bottleneck and puts the subscription cost on the family/EPOA, who are the ones most emotionally invested in the patient's dignity.

### **4\. Create a "Post-Discharge" Safety Net**

Group B (Carer Stress).

* **The Modification:** Add a **"Respite & Support"** button on the profile.  
  * If a person is found wandering and the EPOA is scanned, the profile could provide a "Carer Support" link or a direct line to a pre-arranged respite provider.  
* **Business Edge:** You could partner with private home-care agencies. If a family is struggling (Group B), they can click a button in their Kete ID dashboard to request "Emergency In-Home Support," and you take a referral fee from the provider.

**Updated Kete ID idea:**

1. NFC/QR coded tag. Allows ease of access. No internet required would be ideal as base but will need internet for password protected information. 

2. Form is something someone with dementia would be comfortable using i.e. a brooch, sticker to their mobility aid, sewn on wearable, card in wallet. Needs to be water-proof for showering. Needs to be non-stigmatised from the general public and something that wouldn’t feel foreign and want to be removed.

3. Needs to be uploaded via activated appointed family or the person themselves prior to having incompetence to make their own decisions.  
* The patient *themselves* signs up for Kete ID while they still have capacity. They give explicit consent for their data to be stored and for their "Care Circle" to manage it. This is the cleanest legal route.  
* The family can only upload clinical data if they upload a copy of the **activated EPOA** documents. Your system becomes the "vault" that proves they have the legal right to manage this person’s care.  
* If there is no EPOA, the family can still use Kete ID for **identity and contact info only**. "My name is Joe, please call my wife at \[Number\]" is much less legally sensitive than "Joe has Stage 3 Dementia and takes Seroquel." You could restrict the "Medical/ACP" section of the profile until legal authority (EPOA) is proven.
