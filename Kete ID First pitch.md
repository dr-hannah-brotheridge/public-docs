## **Kete ID Project** 

**Project Lead:** Dr H.Brotheridge  
**Location:** Ōtautahi (Christchurch), Canterbury  
**Stage:** Early pilot development (community-validation phase)

---

### **1\. The Problem**

People living with dementia in New Zealand are frequently admitted to hospital or become involved with emergency services in ways that contradict their care wishes. Confusion in emergencies — whether from wandering, agitation, or acute illness — often results in unnecessary hospital transfers, loss of dignity, and system strain. We have a structural failure across the dementia care ecosystem where rest homes and hospitals are at capacity — clinicians often don’t know a patient’s baseline or advanced care plan, so default to admitting to hospital. That’s costly, disorienting, and often traumatic for the person; especially when it's against their wishes.

Dementia care sits awkwardly between health, social, and emergency services, meaning no one owns the communication problem. It can strip autonomy gradually, and the system can compound that loss by treating the person as an anonymous “confused elderly patient.” The sad reality of it is, the world has more and more people experiencing dementia every day as our population lives longer- and we require new ways of managing this. A dementia-specific ID, like Kete ID reframes the encounter:  
 “This person has an identity, a care plan, and people who support them — here’s how to help.” It’s not so much a medical device as it is a humanising interface.

Current medical ID systems (e.g., MedicAlert) are designed for general medical conditions rather than dementia-specific needs. They lack personalised behavioural and situational context (e.g., preferred contact, usual residence, or advanced care instructions). This creates a critical communication gap between caregivers, emergency responders and health services.

---

### **2\. The Proposed Solution**

The **Kete ID** is a simple NFC/QR-enabled wristband (or an item attached to the person) linking to a secure online profile containing:

* Care preferences and emergency contacts

* Facility or home address for safe return

* Optional advanced care directives \- link to power of attorney (active or not), advanced care plan, DNR decision)

* Brief significant notes for clinicians (e.g., on blood thinners/antipsychotics, responds best to calm tone, prefers low stimulus environments etc)

Eventually this could be linked to a live interface to allow frequently updated medications, any upcoming appointments/reviews and a link to legal documentations by the individual and their consented caregiver. This creates a holistic picture of their dementia management

When scanned by paramedics, care staff or public support the ID provides context-sensitive information that guides decision-making and reduces unnecessary hospitalisation or confusion.

---

### **3\. Expected Benefits**

* Faster identification of people found wandering or presenting to emergency services.  
* Improved communication between caregivers and first responders.  
* Reduced unnecessary hospital transfers.  
* Faster hospital turnover of those individuals with dementia who do not require admissions.  
* Greater dignity and autonomy for people with dementia.  
* Greater holistic understanding of an individual with dementia and how our community can serve their wishes and their caregiver’s needs.

### **4\. Current Stage**

Prototype ID and digital profile in development. Seeking partner facility for a small co-design pilot.

Current feedback in early stages:

- A lot of patients with dementia cannot wear jewellery due to confusion, irritation and many other reasons. Therefore the prototype needs to be modified to be usable in other forms for e.g. brooch on clothes, sewn label, less medical-looking wristband, attached to mobility aids etc where they're more likely to keep it with them.
